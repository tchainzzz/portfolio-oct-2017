Hello. If you're reading this, you've likely been directed here by my resume. You're probably wondering, why is there a random website here, about this "Trenton Chang?" Isn't that a little weird? The truth is, I had just taught myself how to use HTML and CSS, but I had no opportunity to use those skills in real life. I wanted to do something with the knowledge I had just gained, so I thought — why not make a page about myself? So in the small hours of the night, after many web tutorials, downloading some programs, and a cup of coffee, this page was born. It turns out that my knowledge of HTML and CSS wasn't quite as solid as I had thought it was, but I overcame those obstacles to create this page: my personal about page for the world to see.

Tl;dr: I was bored. I taught myself how to make a website about myself.

- Trenton Chang

About Me

My name is Trenton, and I am currently a sophomore at Stanford University. I am majoring in American Studies. I was born in the U.S. to two immigrants from Taiwan. When I was a small child I moved to the small town of Logan, Utah, and it is there that life began for me. I lived there until I went to college at Stanford University. Though my major is American Studies, I am interested in a variety of topics, from music to computer programming to languages. That's why American Studies stood out to me: American Studies is an interdisciplinary field, hinting at the question, "What does it mean to be American?" And that's a loaded question without an answer, but if we approach the question from a variety of fields, we might be able to create something interesting to talk about. That's what I want to make my life about: creating interesting things.

I suppose my passion for creation is pretty clear from my other interests: writing, coding, and most of all, making music. But since you're here, I want to show you these things. Up to here I've been all talk — so if you want to see what I can do, keep scrolling down.

Writing

I'm a writer. Sometime during high school, I'd discovered that writing, a subject I had long reviled in primary school, had become a productive and enjoyable outlet for me to comment on the world. I found that I, nerd as I am, began looking forward to those in-class essays and writing assignments, for they gave me a chance to uniquely express myself and my thoughts through language. I wanted to further that passion, and so, despite my lack of experience in the area, I applied to be the editor-in-chief of the high school newspaper, the Logan Grizzly. Somehow, I got the position. With help from my talented and dedicated team of editors, we produced multiple print editions of the paper and kept an online version running.

In college, I wanted to continue my work in the press, so I became a music writer. I traveled around the Bay Area periodically, covering concerts by Porter Robinson, Madeon, Zedd, and others as a member of the press. Other weeks, I'd write about new songs, social issues in the music world, or simply music itself. My work with the paper was a wonderful way for me to talk about my passions, and helped me strengthen my communication skills. In the future, I'd like to broaden my horizons and talk about other salient issues: politics, social justice, and Asian-American identity.

Music

Music was certainly my first love. As a pianist for 15 years, I can't remember a time before I was sitting at the ivory keys, plinking out tunes happily. And if you'd asked me what I wanted to be a few years ago, I would've replied, with utmost certainty, "A professional pianist." I must've played thousands of hours of music, famous melodies from Bach, Mozart, Rachmaninoff, and every composer in between. Then, I always pushed myself to play this old music in new ways. My piano teacher always reminded me of the question he asked himself when looking for in a great pianist: "Do they have something to say?"

That's part of what drove me to create my own music. As much as I loved classical music, electronic music production was a space in which I could create every sound and feeling imaginable. To paraphrase one of my greatest inspirations, Flume, the greatest thing about making electronic music is that you can create sounds in an instant that have never been heard before — by anyone. My piano keyboard has now been replaced with my computer keyboard, and I find myself with an orchestra literally at my fingertips, ready to play the beautiful symphonies and melodies in my head.

Coding

I was first exposed to coding my freshman year of college. I had no interest in programming prior to taking my first class, but as soon I started coding, I was hooked. It's amazing being able to create interactive programs and other things via a computer — and it always amazes me the depth and variety of possibilities the world of programming has to offer. I can't say I'm the most experienced coder, having started only half a year ago, but I can say that I've developed a passion for the type of problem-solving computer science encourages me to do. If there's something I don't know how to program, if I want to make it, I will figure out how.
